SIR
Motivations
The SIR model groups a population within an epidemic into Susceptibles, Infecteds and Recovereds - allowing a simple but effective model for predicting (and hence mitigating), the spread of a disease. A standard SIR model assumes infection and recovery rates are constant, where in reality these change over time due to factors such as seasons, interventions or behaviours. Given the standard SIR model is:
\[ \frac{dS}{dt} = - \beta \frac{SI}{N}, \quad \frac{dI}{dt} = \beta \frac{SI}{N} - \gamma I, \quad \frac{dR}{dt} = \gamma I \]
The constants \(\beta\) and \(\gamma\) are the infection and recovery rates respectively. Constants allow a basic but narrow set of circumstances to be modelled (and can easily be worked out by hand). However, by changing these to random variables, this allows us to have a Bayesian workflow and incorporate uncertainty, giving a fuller picture into the possible space of outcomes.
Priors
How do we decide what the probability distributions for \(\beta\) and \(\gamma\) should be? Say that for ease, the typical recovery timescale is 10 days, therefore the recovery rate will be \(\gamma \simeq 0.1\) day\(^{-1}\). Then the prior will be
\[ \log \gamma \sim \mathcal{N}(\log 0.1, 0.5^2) \]
Then, if \(R_0\) is the basic reproduction number, where \(R_0 = \frac{\beta}{\gamma}\) is in the range 1–4, then \(\beta\) is 0.1–0.4 through rearranging. Hence the prior is
\[ \log \beta \sim \mathcal{N}(\log 0.25, 0.5^2) \]
It is worth noting that the log-normal distribution was used since \(\beta\) and \(\gamma\) cannot be negative, and as well, the scale invariance of the log function.
Initial conditions and observation noise
We place weak priors on initial prevalence and on measurement noise. For example, with population \(N\),
\[ \log I_0 \sim \mathcal{N}(\log 10,\;0.5^2), \quad S_0 = N - I_0, \quad R_0^{(\mathrm{init})}=0 \]
and model observed counts \(y_t\) (e.g., infected or incidence) with either
\[ y_t \mid I(t) \sim \mathcal{N}(I(t), \sigma^2), \quad \sigma \sim \text{Half-}t_\nu(0, s) \]
or a count model such as Negative Binomial if dispersion is evident.
